# CONTENT_EVIDENCE.md

The citable-evidence layer for page copy and blog content: verified statistics organized by the page they support, each with a source, a usage class, and a verification status. This is separate from CLIENT_FACTS.md (internal business truth) on purpose. Everything here is external research with a shelf life.

Source: two research passes completed July 2026 (the LBC/OCCC data-verification brief and the telehealth and AI-in-mental-health brief). OCCC shares some of this; use only what fits LBC.

## How to use this file (read before publishing any statistic)

- Usage class: (a) = suitable for a service page. (b) = blog or editorial only. Marked per finding.
- Verification discipline for a live medical site: re-confirm every statistic against its primary source and refresh it to the current year before it goes live. A "2023 rate" is often published a year later; pull the current figure at publish time. Prevalence, utilization, and cost numbers move; efficacy and foundational studies are more stable but still cite the year.
- Never publish the flagged items in the "Do not publish" list below.
- Attribute honestly. "According to the CDC" or "a 2019 study in Scientific Reports found" is the register. Never imply a national figure is local, and never imply a study of one population applies to another.
- These are context and credibility, not claims about the practice's own outcomes. Never present research averages as promises about a client's result. No outcome guarantees (see CLIENT_FACTS.md and voice-tone.md).

## Do not publish (unsubstantiated or method-trap figures)

- "72% of Orange County marriages end in divorce" and "California's divorce rate is 60%." Unsubstantiated, appear only on law-firm SEO pages, contradicted by ACS data. Never use.
- Any "official CDC divorce rate for California or Orange County." California is one of five states that does not report divorce to the CDC National Vital Statistics System. There is no such number; anything claiming one is a proxy or invented.
- The "5.88" divorce figure. It is a refined rate per 1,000 married women (BGSU NCFMR), not a population rate; routinely misused.
- FAIR Health metric conflation: do not mix the claim-lines percentage (about 68.9%) with the patients percentage (about 52.1%). They are different metrics from different reports. Pick one metric per page and label it correctly.
- Two secondhand telehealth items pending primary-source confirmation before any use: the Blue Shield of California "62% of urban youth prefer in-person" figure, and the Medicare telehealth shutdown / CAA-2026 timeline. Trace to originals first.

## Marriage and divorce (supports /therapy/couples, /discernment-counseling, and divorce-recovery content)

- National marriage rate 6.1 per 1,000 (2023 provisional); national divorce rate 2.4 per 1,000 (2023 provisional), down from 4.0 in 2000. Source: CDC/NCHS National Marriage and Divorce Rate Trends 2000 to 2023. Honest framing: divorce is declining nationally. Class (a)/(b).
- Best defensible local metric, ACS "percent currently divorced": Orange County about 9.2 percent of adults 15+ vs. California about 8.9 percent (ACS 2021); neighbors ranged LA 8.2 percent to San Diego 9.9 percent. Pull the current-year figure at data.census.gov before publishing. Framing: OC runs very slightly above the state average, not dramatically. Class (a).
- Marriages-vs-divorces framing (national): ACS 2023 showed about 2,361,030 marriages and 992,677 divorces, roughly 2.4 marriages per divorce, the highest ratio since ACS began tracking in 2008. Source: BGSU NCFMR FP-24-27. Class (b).
- OC gray-divorce angle: FLAGGED secondhand (law-firm analysis citing OC Health Care Agency and CA Dept. of Finance): divorce share reportedly peaks at ages 60 to 64, and OC accounts for about 10 percent of California divorces vs. about 8 percent of state population. Confirm against originals before any use. Class (b) only.

## Outdoor, blue-space, and walk-and-talk (supports /beach-therapy, the strongest evidence on the list)

- The "120 minutes" finding: in an analysis of about 19,000 UK respondents, at least 120 minutes per week in natural settings was associated with significantly higher well-being; below that threshold, no better than zero. Consistent across income, proximity, and exercise. Source: White et al., 2019, Scientific Reports (nature.com/articles/s41598-019-44097-3). Anchor: a weekly Beach Therapy session plus a walk or two is roughly this dose. Class (a).
- Coastal blue space specifically outperforms: an 18-country survey (n=16,307) found green, inland-blue, and coastal-blue visits all positively associated with well-being and negatively with distress (White et al., 2021, Scientific Reports, s41598-021-87675-0). A follow-up of about 15,000 blue-space visits found only coastal sites were associated with significantly higher visit happiness, and coastal-visit frequency was more strongly associated with psychological well-being than other environments (s41598-023-28544-w). This is the "why the beach, specifically" evidence. Class (a).
- Systematic-review backing: Gascon et al., 2017 (Int. J. Hygiene and Environmental Health), 35 studies, more positive associations for coastal than freshwater. BlueHealth narrative synthesis, White et al., 2020 (Environmental Research, sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0013935120310665). Class (b) for depth.
- Walk-and-talk specifically: the research is real but young and mostly qualitative. Studies of therapists suggest outdoor therapy is at least as effective as indoor, with some clients benefiting more; physical activity in natural settings is associated with greater revitalization and decreased depression, anxiety, and anger vs. indoor or urban settings. Frame as promising and consistent with the broader nature literature, not as hard efficacy proof. Class (a) with careful framing.

## Telehealth (supports /telehealth; frame around parity, not a growth claim)

- Framing rule: build the telehealth premise on California's permanent payment-parity policy, not on a post-pandemic growth-trajectory claim (the data did not support the growth framing as originally briefed). Class (a).
- California-specific adoption: a RAND/CHCF study of federally qualified health centers documents CA telehealth behavioral-health use. Pull the specific figure and year from the RAND/CHCF report before publishing. Class (a).
- Efficacy: videoconferencing psychotherapy is broadly found non-inferior to in-person (see Fernandez et al., 2021 meta-analysis). Confirm the effect sizes and couples-specific findings before citing precisely. Class (a).
- California AI legislation relevant to telehealth/therapy context: AB 489 and SB 243. Class (b).
- Orange County-specific utilization data does not exist in public sources. If a local utilization claim is needed, the practice's own intake data is the more citable source. Do not manufacture a local number.

## Anxiety, depression, and clinical-approach evidence (supports /therapy/individuals, /specialties/anxiety, /specialties/depression)

- Prevalence: pull the current national anxiety and depression figures from CDC NHIS (2024 or newer), and California and Orange County figures from the California Health Interview Survey (AskCHIS, ask.chis.ucla.edu) at publish time. Do not assume the local number runs above or below the national one; report what the data shows. Class (a).
- Evidence base for the confirmed modalities: CBT is strongly supported for anxiety and depression (Hofmann et al., 2012 meta-analysis is the standard citation); ACT, DBT, and Solution-Focused Brief Therapy also have supporting meta-analytic evidence. Confirm the specific effect sizes and the exact study before quoting numbers. These support the CONFIRMED modalities only (see CLIENT_FACTS.md). Class (a) for the fact that the approaches are evidence-based; (b) for detailed effect sizes.

## Couples outcomes: infidelity, premarital, discernment (supports /specialties/infidelity, /therapy/couples)

## AI in mental health (blog only, class (b), sensitive)

- Therapist adoption: APA 2025 Practitioner Pulse Survey, close to 29 percent of psychologists use AI at least monthly (more than double the prior year); among users, 52 percent writing assistance, 33 percent content, 32 percent summarization, 22 percent notes. Concern is rising in parallel: 67 percent worry about data breaches, at least 60 percent about bias and inaccuracy. Source: apa.org/pubs/reports/practitioner/2025/full-report.pdf. Angle: "AI won't replace your therapist, but it might give them their evenings back."
- Burnout evidence (medical, not therapy-specific): a study of 1,400+ clinicians (Mass General Brigham and Emory) found burnout fell from 52.6 percent to 30.7 percent after 84 days of ambient AI documentation (JAMA Network Open, 2026). Ensora Health "Future of Therapy" (2025, vendor, treat as directional): 40 percent of therapists use AI, 59 percent expect to by 2030, 82 percent report burnout.
- Client skepticism (the strongest blog angle): a YouGov survey found only about 11 percent of Americans open to AI for mental health and about 8 percent trust it; KFF found 77 percent worry about how AI stores health information. On crisis lines, client suspicion of talking to AI rose from 0.8 percent to 2.6 percent (June 2024 to March 2025), and 21.5 percent of suspicious clients said they preferred humans. Pitch: "People will use AI to talk about their feelings, but they don't trust it with their feelings."
- Handle AI-safety incidents and legal cases as editorial-only and with care given the sensitivity.

## Support and crisis resources (a safety pattern, not a statistic)

Therapy content is crisis-adjacent. Crisis-adjacent pages (telehealth, depression, anxiety, grief) and the site footer should carry a short support-resources block, and it must be unmissable. The practice is not a crisis or emergency service (the "Be Seen Within 24 Hours" promise is not a crisis line). Confirm these resources are current at build time; as of 2026 they are accurate:

- If you are in immediate danger or a medical emergency, call 911.
- 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline: call or text 988, or chat at 988lifeline.org. Free, confidential, 24/7.
- Crisis Text Line: text HOME to 741741.
- SAMHSA National Helpline (treatment referrals): 1-800-662-4357.

Copy pattern (warm, in Kay's register, not clinical): make clear the practice cares, that these lines exist for moments that cannot wait, and that reaching out for the free call is welcome when the moment is not an emergency. Build this as a reusable component so every crisis-adjacent page and the footer share the same block.
