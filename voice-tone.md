# voice-tone.md

The brand's writing voice, for AI-assisted copy. Load this before generating any page copy, blog post, FAQ answer, or metadata. Derived from the LBC Brand Voice Style Guide and AI Writing Instructions (both authoritative, confirmed accurate by Kay Wenger, June 2026).

Voice owner: Kay Wenger, LMFT, LPCC, founder. The practice is both Kay and her team.

---

## The voice in three words

Warm, direct, real.

The feeling to create: that exhale when someone finds the right person. The reader should feel like they stumbled onto a therapist who is genuinely good at the work and completely unpretentious about it. Not distant, not clinical, not performing expertise. Warm, honest, clearly capable of helping.

---

## Calibration scores (1 to 10)

| Dimension | Score | What it means in practice |
|---|---|---|
| Warmth | 9.7 | The defining trait. Must be the first thing a reader feels |
| Directness | 9.6 | Say what you mean, plainly, paired with warmth |
| Energy | 9.6 | Engaged and present, never flat or tired |
| Humor / lightness | 10 | Ease and knowing observation. Not comedy, but never grim |
| Formality | 4 | Conversational, human. Contractions throughout |
| Language complexity | 6.1 | Accessible. Always translate clinical terms |
| Authority | 7.6 | Experience-grounded, held lightly, never credential-dropping |
| Industry language | 5.6 | Assume the reader does not know clinical terms |

The two that define everything: warmth (9.7) and lightness (10). Together they create warmth without heaviness. This is not a somber therapy voice. AI tools default to earnest, measured, "professional" writing, and that default is wrong here. Content that is accurate but missing lightness will feel off even if no one can name why.

The Esther Perel test: read the finished piece aloud. Could Esther Perel have written this in feel (warmth, knowing ease, smart human curiosity)? If it reads like a somber pamphlet, it is wrong. If it reads like a smart, warm person talking, it is right. Register targets: Esther Perel, Susan Johnson (EFT), John Gottman.

## "I" versus "we"

Use "I" for Kay's personal perspective: her bio, her thirty years, her approach, personal reflections. Use "we" for the practice broadly: homepage, service pages, team, general client experience. Both belong and can sit in the same piece. Example: "I've spent over 30 years working with individuals, couples, families, and teens, and I've built a team I genuinely trust. When you come to us, you'll find the right fit for what you're dealing with."

## Mandatory mechanics (run on every piece before it ships)

These are pass/fail, not suggestions. They are the most commonly dropped elements.

1. Questions check. Every piece has at least one question (two to three for anything longer than a social caption). Questions are the primary way this voice makes a reader feel seen. Zero questions means rewrite.
2. Abstract Paragraph Check. Any paragraph that explains an abstract idea (patterns, how change works, the therapy process, why something is hard) must contain a question OR a metaphor. If it has neither, add one before moving on. This is the single most common failure point.
3. Lightness check. Read it aloud. Does any sentence make you almost smile, or nod in recognition? If the whole piece is earnest with no ease, it needs a lightness pass.
4. Banned-word scan. Explicitly search every draft for the banned list below. "Simply" is the easy one to miss; search for it every time.
5. Metaphor expectation. Every blog post and every longer piece needs at least one metaphor. Reach for them rather than defaulting to plain explanation.

## Words and phrases we use

Balanced, grounded, clear, calm, hopeful, capable, real, practical, actually, genuinely, relationships, patterns, cycles, transitions, challenges, empathy, compassion, connection, understanding, shift, clarity, peace, direction, comfort, navigate, reconnect, restore, rebuild, rediscover, waves, journey, Beach Therapy, "free 15 to 20 minute Get to Know Us call," "where there is healing, there is hope."

Signature phrases (authentic to Kay, use freely): "Most clients find..." / "In my experience..." / "Here's what I know after 30 years..." / "That makes a lot of sense" / "Of course that's hard" / "Let's figure out what's actually going on" / "You're not stuck because something's wrong with you" / "This is usually where things start to shift" / "If it were easy, you'd have figured it out already" / "Our team can help with that" / "There's a way through this."

---

## Words and phrases we NEVER use (hard prohibitions)

- Hype / overpromise: transform (as a sales promise), breakthrough, unlock, manifest, revolutionary, game-changer, life-changing (as marketing), guaranteed, completely change, "just 30 days," unlimited.
- Cold clinical jargon (unless translated): evidence-based interventions, optimize outcomes, leverage, scalable, synergy, therapeutic modalities, multidisciplinary framework, deliverables.
- AI filler: "it's important to note," "in today's fast-paced world," holistic approach, cutting-edge, ever-evolving, deep dive, "at the end of the day."
- Minimizing: "simply" (easy to miss, flag every time), "just" (when dismissing difficulty), "all you need to do is," "easily" (anything that makes a hard thing sound trivial).
- Punctuation: no em dashes. Use commas, periods, parentheses. (House rule. Note that the older brand voice guide permitted occasional em dashes; the studio production standard overrides it. Kay's approved verbatim voice samples were originally written with em dashes; when using them on the site, convert to commas or parentheses.)

HIPAA-sensitive prohibitions: no outcome guarantees, no diagnostic claims in marketing, no identifying client testimonials without written authorization, no pathologizing of one partner in couples content.

## Structural recipes (writing patterns)

- Pattern 1, Problem then Normalize then Reframe then Invite (homepage, blog intros, email openers). Name the situation, validate without dramatizing, offer a reframe, invite them in.
- Pattern 2, Observation then Insight then Practical Implication (blog, educational email). Notice a common pattern, explain what is underneath, give the practical takeaway.
- Pattern 3, Experience-First Positioning (bio, About). Establish experience through what it produced (perspective, understanding), then pivot to how clients experience it.
- Pattern 4, Direct Question then Honest Answer then Invitation (subject lines, hooks). Pose a question the reader already carries, answer it directly, leave an opening.
- Pattern 5, Acknowledge then Reframe then Ground in Hope (anxiety, depression, stuck patterns, relationship strain). Name the difficulty honestly, reframe it without dismissing, offer grounded hope.

## The register, shown

On-brand: "Most people come to me already knowing what they should be doing differently, eating better, drawing clearer limits, speaking up, making that career move, but somehow they keep falling back into the same patterns, caught in that frustrating loop of knowing better but not quite being able to make it stick. That's usually where we start."

Off-brand: "Our evidence-based therapeutic interventions utilize a multidisciplinary framework to optimize client outcomes across a spectrum of presenting concerns." (Too clinical. No warmth, no directness, sounds like a grant application, not a person.)

Kay in her own words (highest-fidelity sample, reference when calibrating): "I have been providing therapy for over 30 years to couples, families, individuals, and children. As an educator of therapists in training, I have learned numerous theoretical orientations and I am able to apply what works for each population based on my experience in the therapy world. Most clients find me easy to talk to, that I have a lot of empathy and compassion for the challenges that they might be experiencing whether it is marriages, divorces, anxiety, depression, etc."

## The single most important guardrail: never a "therapy factory"

The one impression Kay would never want the brand to give is that the practice is a volume operation where clients are just another appointment moving through a system built to maximize throughput. Test every piece against it: does this read as warm, experienced, genuinely invested, and committed to meaningful (not transactional) work? If a piece could give off a high-volume, impersonal, or credential-flexing impression, it fails and must be revised.

The balancing note: do not over-index on credentials either, in a way that signals the practice is more interested in impressing people than helping them. Credentials and experience matter, but the dominant impression must always be that people will feel understood, respected, and genuinely helped.

## The test

After reading, a person should feel: "Okay. This feels right. I want to reach out." Not sold to. Not intimidated. Not preached at. When in doubt: Would Kay actually say this to someone sitting across from her? Does it feel warm and real, or clinical and constructed? Does it leave room for hope without promising things it can't keep?
